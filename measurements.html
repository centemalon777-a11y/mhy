Introduction

Measurement is the process of finding the size, length, amount, or degree of something by comparing it with a standard unit. In physics, accurate measurements are essential to describe quantities and perform experiments correctly.

Physical Quantities

Physical quantities are properties of matter that can be measured. They are divided into:

  • Fundamental (Base) Quantities: Cannot be derived from other quantities (e.g., length, mass, time, temperature, electric current, luminous intensity, amount of substance).
  • Derived Quantities: Obtained from base quantities (e.g., velocity, force, pressure, density).

Accuracy, Precision, and Errors

  • Accuracy: How close a measurement is to the true value.
  • Precision: How consistent repeated measurements are.
  • Errors: The difference between measured and true values. Errors can be systematic, random, or due to instrument limitations.

Measuring Instruments

  • Ruler and metre rule ‚Üí for length.
  • Vernier caliper ‚Üí for small lengths and diameters (accuracy 0.01 cm).
  • Micrometer screw gauge ‚Üí for very small dimensions (accuracy 0.01 mm).
  • Stopwatch/clock ‚Üí for time.
  • Balance ‚Üí for mass.
  • Thermometer ‚Üí for temperature.

Significant Figures

The number of meaningful digits in a measurement. They indicate the precision of the instrument used. Example: 25.3 cm has 3 significant figures.

Conclusion

Measurement is fundamental to physics. Without it, scientific theories cannot be tested, and technological progress would not be possible. Accurate instruments, proper use of units, and error management are crucial in experiments.
