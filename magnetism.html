Introduction

Magnetism is a force of attraction or repulsion that acts between materials due to the motion of electric charges. It is closely related to electricity and forms the basis of electromagnetism. Natural magnets like lodestone were discovered thousands of years ago, and today magnetism plays an important role in science and technology.

Properties of Magnets

  • Magnets have two poles: North (N) and South (S).
  • Like poles repel, unlike poles attract.
  • Magnetic poles always exist in pairs; they cannot be isolated.
  • Magnets produce a magnetic field around them.

Magnetic Field

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where magnetic force can be felt. The field is represented by lines of force (magnetic flux lines).

  • They emerge from the north pole and enter the south pole.
  • They never intersect.
  • The closer the lines, the stronger the field.

Types of Magnets

  • Natural Magnets: Found in nature (e.g., lodestone).
  • Artificial Magnets: Made by humans, such as bar magnets, horseshoe magnets.
  • Temporary Magnets: Magnetized for a short time.
  • Permanent Magnets: Retain magnetism for a long period.

Magnetization

The process of making a material magnetic is called magnetization. Methods include:

  • Stroking with a magnet.
  • Electrical method (passing current through a coil).
  • Induction method (placing near a strong magnet).

Magnetic Materials

  • Ferromagnetic: Strongly attracted to magnets (iron, cobalt, nickel).
  • Paramagnetic: Weakly attracted (aluminum, platinum).
  • Diamagnetic: Weakly repelled (copper, bismuth).

Applications of Magnetism

  • In compasses for navigation.
  • In electric motors and generators.
  • In transformers and electromagnets.
  • In magnetic storage devices (hard drives, tapes).
  • In medical imaging (MRI machines).

Conclusion

Magnetism is a fundamental concept that explains many natural and technological phenomena. It is the basis of modern electrical engineering, telecommunications, and everyday devices.
