🌡️ Heat and Temperature

SS1 Physics – Comprehensive Notes

Introduction

Heat is a form of energy that is transferred from one body to another due to a difference in temperature. Temperature is the measure of the degree of hotness or coldness of a body.

Difference Between Heat and Temperature

  • Heat is energy in transit; temperature is a measure of average kinetic energy of particles.
  • Heat is measured in Joules (J); temperature is measured in Celsius (°C), Kelvin (K), or Fahrenheit (°F).
  • Heat depends on mass, temperature, and material; temperature does not depend on mass.

Measurement

  • Temperature is measured with a thermometer (e.g., mercury-in-glass, digital thermometer).
  • Heat is measured indirectly using the principle of calorimetry.

Heat Capacity and Specific Heat Capacity

  • Heat Capacity (C): The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a body by 1 K.
    C = Q / ΔT
  • Specific Heat Capacity (c): The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K.
    Q = mcΔT
    where
    Q = heat energy (J)
    m = mass (kg)
    c = specific heat capacity (J/kg·K)
    ΔT = change in temperature

Change of State

When heat is supplied to a substance, it may cause a change of state instead of a rise in temperature.

  • Latent Heat: The heat absorbed or released during a change of state without a change in temperature.
  • Latent Heat of Fusion: Heat required to change 1 kg of a solid to liquid at constant temperature.
  • Latent Heat of Vaporization: Heat required to change 1 kg of a liquid to gas at constant temperature.
Q = mL
where L = latent heat (J/kg).

Methods of Heat Transfer

  • Conduction – Transfer of heat through solids by particle vibration.
  • Convection – Transfer of heat in fluids by the actual movement of particles.
  • Radiation – Transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves without a medium.

Example Problem

Example: Calculate the heat required to raise the temperature of 2 kg of water from 20°C to 80°C. (Specific heat capacity of water = 4200 J/kg·K)

Q = mcΔT = 2 × 4200 × (80 – 20) = 2 × 4200 × 60 = 504,000 J

→ The heat required is 504 kJ.

Applications of Heat

  • Cooking food.
  • Generating electricity in thermal power stations.
  • Refrigeration and air conditioning.
  • Sterilization and medical uses.

Conclusion

Heat and temperature are closely related but distinct concepts in physics. Understanding them is essential for explaining everyday phenomena like boiling water, melting ice, and weather patterns.
